abstract: |
  The contribution of this study is to propose a continuum-damage mechanics-like (CDM-like) constitutive law by combining a cohesive zone model and a ductie fracture model to realize change of fracture behavior at ductile-to-brittle transition (DBT) region. In low temperature, brittle cracks rapidly propagate in the parallel direction to cleavage plane without the plastic deformation due to the increase of yield strength in contrast to the decrease of fracture toughness. The change of stress release process due to the propagation of brittle cracks is represented throughout the local equiliblium state between principal stresses and cohesive tractions that are determined by a cohesive zone model. On the other hand, in room temperature, ductile cracks develop from the nucleation, growth and coalescence of the voids along with plastic deformation. Development of ductile cracks is realized by the ductile fracture model as the shrinkage of yield surface along with the increase of voids. Moreover, in DBT temperature,ductile cracks initially develop and turn into brittle cracks as a result of the decrease of fracture toughness during the ductile fracture, which leads to the decrease in cross-sectional area of the specimen by the nucleation and growth of the voids. In our proposed constitutive law, DBT behavior is represented by incorperating void volume fraction into the critical energy release rate of a cohesive zone model. Additionally, the change of plastic hardening in low, room, and DBT temperatures are approximated by Voce hardening law which depends on temperature. The capability of our proposed law is demonstrated by comparison with experimental results of compact tension specimen in low, room, and DBT temperatures.
